Washington, DC energy benchmarking guide

By Quoin Editorial Team Last updated March 27, 2026

Washington, DC requires covered buildings to report annual energy and water performance through ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager. This guide explains who is covered, what data you need, how Portfolio Manager fits the process, and how to avoid common reporting errors before May 1.

At a glance

Current District timeline checkpoints

Private covered buildings Over 10,000 square feet.
Annual reporting deadline May 1.
Next verification deadline May 1, 2027 for calendar year 2026 data.
Reporting platform ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager.
Definition

What DC energy benchmarking is

DC energy benchmarking is the annual reporting requirement for covered buildings to submit energy and water performance through ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager.

Coverage

Who is covered

Current District guidance says private buildings over 10,000 square feet are covered and must benchmark annually.

Inputs

What data you need

  • Building identity and core property details.
  • Utility account coverage and meter mapping.
  • Whole-building energy and water usage.
  • Operational characteristics required for reporting.
  • Documentation for anomalies and unusual values.
Platform

How Portfolio Manager works in DC

Portfolio Manager is the reporting platform used for DC benchmarking. It is where data gets reported, but not where the annual operational mess gets solved. The heavy work is usually gathering records, resolving gaps, and validating the benchmark before submission.

Verification of scope

How to check the covered building list

Start by confirming whether the building meets the current coverage threshold and whether the team has current ownership of the reporting process. If there is any ambiguity, align your internal scope list to the latest District guidance before collecting data.

Workflow

A practical workflow for owners, operators, and consultants

  1. Confirm which buildings are covered.
  2. Assemble building and utility records early.
  3. Check account coverage and missing periods.
  4. Run QA on outliers, inconsistencies, and implausible values.
  5. Document assumptions and unresolved items.
  6. Prepare for Portfolio Manager submission and later verification questions.
What changed

Why the District timeline can still look inconsistent

Current guidance uses a May 1 annual deadline and a verification cycle that next returns on May 1, 2027 for calendar year 2026 data. Older official snippets still circulate with April 1 and a three-year verification cadence.

That conflict is exactly why teams should treat benchmarking as the annual record and use the current deadline and verification articles as the live source of truth. Broader BEPS timing should be treated as a related but separate planning layer.

Common mistakes

Where teams usually lose time

  • Starting the process too close to the filing window.
  • Discovering account coverage problems after data has already been loaded.
  • Failing to document why unusual values were accepted.
  • Treating Portfolio Manager as the workflow instead of the reporting endpoint.
FAQ

Frequently asked guide questions

How do I know if my building is covered?

Current District guidance says private buildings over 10,000 square feet are covered.

What data do I need before I start?

You need building details, utility coverage, annual usage data, and enough documentation to explain anomalies.

Is Portfolio Manager required?

Yes. It is the reporting platform used for DC benchmarking.

What is third-party verification?

It is a separate validation requirement that next returns on May 1, 2027 for calendar year 2026 data.

Why does the DC deadline look different on older pages?

Older materials reflect the pre-amendment rule. Current guidance uses the May 1 deadline and the six-year verification cadence.

How can Quoin reduce last-minute reporting work?

By making missing data, account gaps, and QA issues visible earlier, before filing week compresses every decision.
